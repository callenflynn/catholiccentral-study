Introduction to Navigation

Navigation is knowing where you are, where you're going, and how to get there. Aviation navigation requires maps, direction-indicating instruments, and careful planning since pilots cannot stop to ask for directions. This chapter covers basic navigation principles that all pilots must learn and practice.

Maps and Map Projections

Global Coordinate System

  • Great circles: Circles passing through Earth's center
  • Small circles: All other circles
  • Graticule: Grid system with 18 primary north-south lines (longitude/meridians) and east-west lines (latitude/parallels) spaced 10 degrees apart
  • Prime meridian: 0¬∞ longitude through Greenwich, England; divides Eastern/Western hemispheres
  • Equator: 0¬∞ latitude; divides Northern/Southern hemispheres
  • Coordinates: Read latitude first (N/S), then longitude (E/W). Example: 36¬∞N 84¬∞W
  • Distance measurement: Each degree has 60 minutes; 1 minute = 1 nautical mile (6,076 feet)

Map Projections

Flattening Earth's sphere onto flat paper causes distortion:

  • Mercator: Best for small areas where Earth appears flat
  • Lambert-Conformal: Cone wrapped around globe; better for longer distances; great circles appear nearly straight; used for sectional charts

Sectional Aeronautical Charts

Most popular charts for small aircraft on short flights. Lambert-Conformal projections with symbols and colors representing terrain and features.

Relief (Elevation)

  • Color tints: 1,000-foot increments (green = 0-1,000 ft, etc.); irregular borders show terrain roughness
  • Contour lines: Connect equal elevations; spacing shows slope steepness (wide = gradual, close = steep, together = cliff)
  • Highest points: Black dots with elevation numbers
  • Pictorial shading: Airbrush makes mountains look 3D

Hydrographic Features

Water shown in blue (lakes, streams). Valuable for navigation since each lake has unique, identifiable shape.

Cultural Features

  • Large cities: Bright yellow with black border
  • Small communities: Small black circles
  • Infrastructure: Mines, highways (black/magenta lines), railroads (lines with crossties), power lines
  • Obstructions: Clearly marked with heights

Civil Airports

Symbols indicate runway type and facilities:

  • Unpaved: Magenta circle
  • With facilities (fuel, food, lodging): Four tick marks from circle
  • Private: "R" in circle; "(Pvt)" after name
  • Paved 1,500-8,000 ft: Magenta disk (no tower) or blue disk (tower)
  • Runways 8,000+ ft: Heavily outlined; magenta (no tower) or blue (tower)

Airspace and Airways

Controlled Airspace

Subject to FAA air traffic control:

  • Continental control area: Starts at 14,500 ft MSL, extends upward, covers entire US
  • Control zones: Around airports, surface to continental control area
  • Airport traffic area: Within control zones with operating towers

Special-Use Airspace

  • Prohibited: Must avoid
  • Restricted: Prohibited at certain times
  • Warning/Alert areas: Exercise extreme caution
  • MOA (Military Operations Area): Military training; higher collision risk
  • MTR (Military Training Route): Low-level training routes

Basic Navigation Principles

True Course vs. Magnetic Course

  • True course: Straight line drawn on chart; direction measured clockwise from longitude line
  • Magnetic course: Adjusted for magnetic variation using isogonic lines on charts
  • Compass deviation: Aircraft metals and electrical systems cause additional compass errors

True Airspeed vs. Ground Speed

  • True airspeed: Speed through air mass; measured by aircraft instruments
  • Ground speed: Speed across Earth's surface; determines time to destination
  • Wind effects: Tailwind increases ground speed; headwind decreases it
  • Wind triangle: Tool to calculate drift correction and wind compensation

Navigation Techniques

Pilotage

Navigating by visible landmarks:

  • Draw true-course line on chart and measure distance
  • Select landmarks as checkpoints (predetermined time/position points)
  • Choose brackets (easy-to-see features on each side of course)
  • Observe wind drift and compensate by changing heading

Dead Reckoning

Professional-level navigation with systematic consideration of all factors:

  1. Draw TC line: Measure direction and distance
  2. Estimate true airspeed: Account for altitude effects (2% increase per 1,000 ft)
  3. Check weather: Wind direction and speed
  4. Determine wind correction: Calculate heading adjustment and ground speed
  5. Find magnetic heading: Apply magnetic variation from isogonic lines
  6. Find compass heading: Apply compass deviation from aircraft card
  7. Calculate time en route: Distance √∑ ground speed
  8. Calculate fuel usage: Fuel flow √ó time
  9. Select checkpoints: Identifiable features along route
  10. File flight plan: With FAA for search and rescue if overdue

Electronic Aids

VOR (Very-High-Frequency Omnidirectional Radio Range)

  • Broadcasts 360¬∞ circle of signals (radials)
  • Course Deviation Indicator (CDI) shows position relative to selected radial
  • Can obtain position fix with two VOR receivers
  • Allows navigation without seeing ground

ADF (Automatic Direction Finder)

  • Needle points toward tuned Non-directional Beacon (NDB)
  • Doesn't compensate for wind (may fly curved path)
  • Signals can bounce off terrain giving incorrect information

DME (Distance-Measuring Equipment)

  • Works with VORTAC stations (VOR + TACAN)
  • Measures line-of-sight distance to station
  • Combined with VOR gives precise position (radial + distance)

LORAN (Long-Range Navigation)

  • Master and slave ground stations broadcast timed signals
  • Aircraft receiver measures time differences in microseconds
  • Special LORAN charts show time differences as curved lines
  • Being replaced by GPS (less expensive, more accurate)

GPS (Global Positioning System)

  • ~30 satellites orbiting Earth + ground tracking stations
  • Needs signals from 4 satellites for: latitude, longitude, elevation, time
  • Differential GPS provides very accurate positioning with ground correction stations
  • Two signals: military and civilian use

INS (Inertial Navigation System)

  • Self-contained unit requiring only electrical power
  • Three accelerometers detect movement in all directions
  • Tracks position based on starting point and all movements
  • Accuracy degrades ~¬Ω nm per hour (drift)

RNAV (Area Navigation System)

  • Computer-controlled navigation using VOR stations or GPS
  • Allows direct flight without following airways
  • Useful for long great-circle courses (saves time and fuel)
  • GPS-based RNAV backed up by INS or ground stations

Landing Navigation Systems

Highly accurate systems within limited range for precision approaches to runways.

ILS (Instrument Landing System)

  • Ground components: Localizer (lateral guidance) and glide slope (vertical guidance)
  • Marker beacons: Outer (blue), middle (amber), inner (white) position checkpoints
  • Aircraft equipment: Receivers display localizer and glide-slope needles
  • Precision: Can guide aircraft to 50-foot wide runways in low visibility

MLS (Microwave Landing System)

  • Wide electronic funnel: 120¬∞ horizontal, 20¬∞ vertical spread
  • Easy to enter from any angle within range
  • Precision back-course guidance for go-around procedures
  • Limited adoption; abandoned for land-based US applications

GPS Landing Systems

  • Approaches can include curved paths and tiered altitudes
  • Computer-generated approaches shaped for environmental needs
  • Differential GPS enables precision landing on moving platforms
  • Can enable mid-air refueling rendezvous and UAV operations

Chapter 9 Key Takeaways

  • Navigation Foundation: Understanding global coordinates, map projections, and chart symbols
  • Sectional Charts: Reading relief, hydrographic, cultural features, and airport symbols

  • Course Calculations: True course, magnetic variation, compass deviation, and wind corrections
  • Pilotage Technique: Visual navigation using landmarks, checkpoints, and brackets
  • Dead Reckoning: Systematic 10-step process for professional flight planning
  • Electronic Navigation: VOR, ADF, DME, LORAN, GPS, INS, and RNAV systems
  • Precision Landing: ILS, MLS, and GPS approach systems for safe runway guidance
  • Backup Proficiency: Maintaining basic navigation skills despite electronic advances
  • Complete Process: From departure planning to precision landing approach systems
