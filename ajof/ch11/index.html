Chapter 11 - Air Carriers

Commercial airlines operate as regulated common carriers, transporting passengers and cargo on scheduled routes. Understand the different types of air carriers and the industry transformation following deregulation.

What Are Air Carriers?

Air carriers are commercial airlines that transport passengers and cargo on regular schedules as a business. They're considered "common carriers" (like railroads and bus lines) and are heavily regulated by the Federal Aviation Administration (FAA) to ensure public safety.

Three Main Categories

1. Large Air Carriers

  • Largest airlines by passenger volume
  • Typically carry 100-500+ passengers
  • Fly long-range routes between major cities
  • Examples: Boeing 747, 777, 767, 737, and Airbus A300, A330, A380 series

2. Cargo Carriers

  • Primarily transport freight
  • Now also permitted to carry passengers
  • Use modified passenger aircraft with cargo-handling equipment
  • Major players: FedEx, UPS using hub-and-spoke distribution systems
  • Aircraft: Boeing 747F can carry 200,000 pounds over 3,500 miles

3. Regional and Commuter Carriers

  • Serve smaller cities and limited geographical areas
  • Average trip: several hundred miles with ~20 passengers
  • Use smaller aircraft: twin-engine turboprops or reciprocating engines
  • Examples: Beechcraft 99, DeHavilland Twin Otter, Embraer aircraft

The Game-Changer: Airline Deregulation Act of 1978

Before 1978

  • Civil Aeronautics Board (CAB) strictly controlled fares and routes
  • Airlines needed approval to change prices or add new routes
  • Limited competition

After 1978

  • Airlines gained freedom to enter new routes without approval
  • Competition increased, leading to lower fares on some routes
  • Airlines abandoned unprofitable small-city routes
  • Industry grew from 36 scheduled airlines (1978) to 76 airlines (1988)

Impact on Established Airlines

Older airlines faced severe challenges:

  • Labor costs: Newer airlines were non-union with lower wages
  • Fuel crisis: Operating costs tripled due to energy crisis
  • High interest rates: 15-20% interest on new aircraft loans during late 1970s inflation
  • Competition: New airlines bought cheaper used aircraft
  • Recession: Reduced passenger numbers in early 1980s
  • Air traffic controller strike: Limited flights to many areas

Major Aircraft Manufacturers

United States

  • Boeing: 747, 767, 777, 787 Dreamliner, 737, 757, 727
  • McDonnell-Douglas (now Boeing): DC-10, MD-11, DC-9, MD-80/90

International

  • Airbus (European consortium): A300, A310, A320, A330, A340, A350, A380
  • Lockheed: L-1011 Tristar (production ended 1984)

Network Systems

Point-to-Point

Direct flights with multiple intermediate stops.

Advantages:

  • Low travel time
  • Simpler air traffic control

Disadvantages:

  • Requires high demand
  • Maintenance spread across cities

Hub-and-Spoke

Central hub with flights radiating outward like spokes. Developed after long-range jets were introduced (1950s-60s) and expanded dramatically after deregulation.

Advantages:

  • Greater flexibility and economies of scale
  • Serves smaller cities
  • Consolidated maintenance
  • More destination options for passengers

Disadvantages:

  • Weather disruptions affect entire system
  • Complex air traffic control
  • Large infrastructure investment required

Key Statistics

  • 70% of jet passengers fly trips under 2 hours duration
  • Boeing 747-8: 250 feet long, can carry 500 passengers
  • Commuter aircraft: typically operate from runways as short as 2,000-3,000 feet
  • Air cargo competitive advantage: speed of delivery over distances exceeding 500 miles

Historical Note

Most airlines began as point-to-point operations in the 1940s-50s, similar to rail passenger services. The hub-and-spoke system evolved with jet aircraft, with Chicago O'Hare operating as a major hub by the 1960s.
