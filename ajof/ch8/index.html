Three-Dimensional Movement

Aircraft, spacecraft and even submarines are capable of moving in all possible directions. If one considers such craft to be suspended at the center of an imaginary sphere, the vehicles can move in any direction away from the center of the sphere, and aircraft and spacecraft can roll and spin toward any of the possible directions. This movement is in three dimensions.

Automobiles, trains and surface ships are restricted to two-dimensional movement because they move over the earth's surface only. Everyone is familiar with control over the motion of an automobile. The driver can speed it up, slow it down or stop it. A car's direction can be controlled by changing the direction toward which its wheels point, but it cannot go above or below the road. A train's movement is restricted by the rails over which it must travel.

The airplane, submarine, and spacecraft are the only true three-dimensional machines because of their complete freedom of motion. They can move in any of three axes - what they call six degrees of freedom: Roll, Pitch, Yaw, and heave, surge, sway.

The Basic Airplane and Its Parts

A typical airplane demonstrates the standard parts of a fixed wing aircraft. The fuselage is the basic structure to which all other parts are attached. Even the engine is attached to the fuselage, which is true for most single-engine aircraft. The primary source of lift is the wing, with its attached ailerons. The tail, or empennage, consists of the horizontal stabilizer with its elevators and the vertical stabilizer with its rudder.

Three Axes of an Aircraft

The Longitudinal (Roll) Axis

Running from the tip of the nose to the tip of the tail is the longitudinal axis. Aircraft roll is motion about this axis and results from aileron movement. The ailerons are attached so that one deflects downward when the other deflects upward. When an aileron is not perfectly aligned with the wing, it changes the wing's lift characteristics due to camber changes. With one wing aileron deflected upward and the other downward, lift increases on one side and decreases on the other, resulting in rolling motion around the longitudinal axis.

The Lateral (Pitch) Axis

An imaginary line running from one wingtip through the fuselage to the other wingtip forms the lateral or pitch axis. The airplane pitches its nose up or down about this axis through elevator movement. Moving the control column backward raises the elevator, changing the stabilizer's shape to pull the tail down and pitch the nose up. Some aircraft use a stabilator instead of separate stabilizer and elevator. The entire horizontal tail surface moves to change the angle of attack. This design originated during supersonic flight testing when regular elevators became ineffective at high speeds.

The Vertical (Yaw) Axis

The vertical or yaw axis passes vertically through the meeting point of the longitudinal and lateral axes. Aircraft yaw is motion left or right around this axis, controlled by the rudder. When the pilot presses rudder pedals, the rudder deflects and creates a cambered surface that causes the nose to point in the desired direction.

Aircraft Engines

With few exceptions, aircraft are propelled by engines. The two common types are reciprocating engines and gas turbine engines. Other types include ramjet, scramjet, rocket and electric engines.

Reciprocating Engines

Reciprocating engines use pistons that move back and forth in straight-line motion, which is converted to rotary motion to turn propellers. The most vital parts are cylinders, pistons, connecting rods, crankshaft, valves, spark plugs and a valve-operating mechanism called a cam. The engine operates on the Otto Cycle with five events: intake stroke, compression stroke, ignition and power stroke, and exhaust stroke. Each piston makes four strokes to complete one cycle, occurring thousands of times per minute.

Gas Turbine Engines

Gas turbine engines use hot flowing gases striking turbine blades. There are four basic types: turbojet, turbofan, turboprop, and propfan. These engines have advantages over reciprocating engines - they fly at higher altitudes and speeds, have less vibration, simpler cooling, and produce more thrust per pound of weight.

Turbine engines take in air at the intake and accelerate it to extremely high velocities through the exhaust nozzle. The five basic sections are inlet, compressor, burner, turbine and exhaust.

Turbofan engines have gained popularity because they pull much more air into the engine than simple turbojets. They're quieter and more fuel-efficient than turbojets. Turboprop engines combine gas turbine efficiency with propeller effectiveness, using a turbine to turn a propeller through reduction gears.

Flight Controls

The Wright brothers contributed the first sustained, controlled, powered flight. Control is accomplished through control surfaces: ailerons for roll, elevators for pitch, and rudder for yaw control. Modern computer-controlled flight systems allow control of aircraft that would otherwise be unstable, correcting faster than human pilots could manage.

Lift Enhancing Devices

  • Flaps: Attach to the wing's trailing edge. When lowered, they increase both lift and drag by increasing wing camber. This allows steeper descents and slower landing speeds.
  • Slats and Slots: Protrude from the leading edge. They speed up airflow through openings, helping maintain smooth airflow at high angles of attack.
  • Spoilers: Work to destroy lift by disrupting laminar airflow over portions of the wing. They allow steep descents and can be deployed or retracted quickly.

Aircraft Structure

The fuselage, meaning "to streamline," contains crew, passengers and cargo while withstanding forces from airfoils and landing gear. Three structural types exist: truss, semimonocoque, and monocoque.

  • Truss structures: Use welded tubing with longerons and struts
  • Semimonocoque construction: Uses internal braces to help the skin carry loads
  • True monocoque: Depends entirely on the skin for strength but is uncommon

Landing Gear

Three arrangements are common: conventional with two front wheels and a tailwheel, tricycle with a nosewheel and two main wheels, and tandem with two sets of wheels located one behind the other. Landing gear can be fixed or retractable. Retractable gear reduces drag for higher speeds but adds complexity and cost.

Aircraft Systems

Fuel Systems

Fuel systems deliver fuel from tanks to engines through fuel lines. Two feed systems exist: gravity feed using gravity flow, and force-feed using pumps. All tanks need filler caps, vents for expansion, drains for water removal, and strainers to keep sediment out.

Hydraulic Systems

Based on Pascal's law, hydraulic systems use pressurized fluid to operate brakes, landing gear, flight controls, and flaps. They provide mechanical advantage, allowing pilots to control heavy aircraft systems.

Electrical Systems

Generators provide electricity to charge batteries, start engines, and operate radios, lights, and instruments. Modern aircraft depend heavily on electrical systems for critical functions.

Aircraft Instruments

Early aviators relied on basic senses - wind force for airspeed, visual references for navigation. As aircraft became more capable, instruments became necessary. Instruments are classified by use - performance instruments show aircraft response, control instruments show device conditions. They're also classified by operation: mechanical, pressure, and electrical instruments.

Flight Instruments

Key flight instruments include the airspeed indicator measuring speed through air pressure, altimeter showing altitude based on atmospheric pressure, and attitude indicator providing an artificial horizon using gyroscopic principles. The turn coordinator shows direction and rate of turn plus turn quality. The vertical velocity indicator displays climb or descent rates.

Engine Instruments

The tachometer shows engine speed in RPM and total operating hours. Oil pressure and temperature gauges monitor engine health with color-coded safe operating ranges.

Navigation Instruments

The magnetic compass remains the most basic and reliable navigation instrument. Heading indicators use gyroscopes for primary heading reference. Modern systems include horizontal situation indicators and moving map displays.

Chapter 8 Key Takeaways

  • Three-Dimensional Freedom: Aircraft can move in all six degrees of freedom unlike surface vehicles
  • Aircraft Axes: Longitudinal (roll), lateral (pitch), and vertical (yaw) control aircraft movement
  • Engine Types: Reciprocating and gas turbine engines each have specific advantages and applications
  • Control Systems: Ailerons, elevators, and rudders provide precise aircraft control
  • Aircraft Structure: Fuselage design balances strength, weight, and functionality
  • Critical Systems: Fuel, hydraulic, and electrical systems enable safe aircraft operation
  • Instrumentation Evolution: From basic wind gauges to sophisticated electronic displays

Evolution of Flight: Aircraft instruments have evolved from simple wind gauges and string attitude indicators to sophisticated electronic displays, but all serve the fundamental purpose of providing pilots critical information for safe flight.
