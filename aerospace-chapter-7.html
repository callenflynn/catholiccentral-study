Chapter 7: Basic Aeronautics and Aerodynamics Summary

Key Definitions

  • Aeronautics: The science and art of flight through the atmosphere
  • Aerodynamics: The science relating to the energy of air in motion

Properties of Air

The atmosphere consists of one-fifth oxygen and four-fifths nitrogen, with several key properties:

Pressure

  • Decreases with altitude (like blankets in a pile)
  • Sea level standard: 14.7 psi or 29.92 inches mercury
  • Higher altitudes = less pressure

Temperature

  • Decreases ~3.5°F per 1,000 feet of altitude
  • Continues up to about 38,000 feet MSL
  • Affects air density and aircraft performance

Density

  • Number of molecules in a given volume
  • Lower at higher altitudes and higher temperatures
  • Directly affects aircraft performance (hot, high altitude = poor performance)

Viscosity

  • Air's resistance to flow
  • Creates viscous drag when objects move through air
  • Causes laminar (smooth) vs. turbulent (rough) airflow patterns

Speed of Sound

  • Varies with temperature (761 mph at 59°F, 692 mph at -30°F)
  • Mach 1 = speed of sound
  • Chuck Yeager broke the sound barrier in 1947 with the Bell X-1

Airfoil Design Components

Leading Edge

  • First part to meet relative wind
  • Sharp for high-speed aircraft, rounded for general aviation

Camber

  • Curvature determining thickness
  • Positive camber curves away from centerline
  • Negative camber curves toward centerline

Trailing Edge

  • Where upper and lower surfaces meet at the rear

Chord

  • Imaginary line connecting leading and trailing edges
  • Reference for angle of attack measurements

Key Flight Concepts

Angle of Attack

  • Angle between chord line and relative wind
  • Different from angle of incidence (fixed to aircraft centerline)
  • Critical for lift generation

Relative Wind

  • Airflow opposite to flight path
  • Created by aircraft movement through air

Four Forces of Flight

1. Lift

  • Acts perpendicular to relative wind
  • Induced Lift: From low pressure above wing (camber effect)
  • Dynamic Lift: From high pressure below wing (angle of attack effect)
  • Explained by Bernoulli's Principle: as fluid speed increases, pressure decreases

2. Weight

  • Always acts toward gravity
  • Must be managed through:
    • Lightweight materials (aluminum alloys, composites)
    • Weight limitations (maximum gross weight)
    • Proper weight distribution (affects center of gravity)

3. Thrust

  • Propels aircraft forward
  • Can be vectored for enhanced maneuverability
  • Must overcome drag for forward motion

4. Drag

Multiple types exist:

  • Friction Drag: Air particles rubbing against aircraft surfaces
  • Form Drag: Created by aircraft shape and turbulence
  • Induced Drag: Component of lift vector pointing backward
  • Wave Drag: From shock waves at high speeds

Real-World Effects

Turbulence

  • Air scraping against rough surfaces
  • Reduces airfoil efficiency
  • Creates boundary layer separation

Stalls

  • Occurs when airflow separates from wing surface
  • Loss of lift-producing low pressure above wing
  • Can lead to loss of control

Wingtip Vortices

  • Swirling air masses at wingtips
  • Result from spanwise airflow joining
  • Create wake turbulence

Supersonic Flight

Characteristics

  • Air becomes compressible at high speeds
  • Creates shock waves when aircraft exceeds Mach 1
  • Compression waves: Air pushed aside at leading edges
  • Expansion waves: Air filling in at trailing edges

Challenges

  • Wave drag from shock waves
  • Different design requirements than subsonic aircraft
  • Aircraft must still operate subsonically for takeoff/landing

Balanced Flight

In straight-and-level, unaccelerated flight:

  • Lift = Weight
  • Thrust = Drag
  • Any imbalance causes acceleration, climbing, or turning

Practical Applications

  • Weight and balance calculations using useful load
  • Thrust vectoring for enhanced maneuverability
  • Streamlining to reduce drag
  • Speed brakes for controlled deceleration
  • Winglets to reduce induced drag
