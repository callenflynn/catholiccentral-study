Summary of "Man or Rabbit" by C.S. Lewis

In "Man or Rabbit," C.S. Lewis tackles the question: "Can't you lead a good life without believing in Christianity?" But Lewis quickly points out that this question misses the real point entirely.

Lewis argues that asking this question shows the wrong attitude from the start. The person asking it seems to be saying, "I don't care if Christianity is actually true or not - I just want to know if it's useful for being good." Lewis finds this troubling because one of the things that makes humans different from animals is our desire to know what's actually real, not just what's convenient.

Christianity Makes Claims About Reality

According to Lewis, Christianity isn't just a self-help program or moral guide - it claims to tell us what the universe is actually like. Either these claims are true or they're false, and if we're honest people, we should care about finding out which one it is. If Christianity is false, an honest person wouldn't want to believe it just because it's helpful. If it's true, an honest person would want to believe it even if it didn't help at all.

Different Worldviews Lead to Different Actions

Lewis explains that Christians and non-Christians (he calls them "Materialists") might both want to help people, but they'll do it differently because they have completely different ideas about what humans are and what life is about.

A Christian believes people live forever, were created by God, and can only find true happiness through God. A Materialist believes humans are just accidents of nature who live about 70 years and can be made happy through good government and social programs. These different beliefs will lead to different ideas about education, justice, and what's most important in life.

The Real Question

Lewis says the real question isn't whether someone can live a good life without Christianity - obviously some non-Christians have been good people. The real question is whether you can, especially if you've heard about Christianity and are wondering if it might be true.

Lewis is particularly critical of people who avoid seriously looking into Christianity because they're afraid of what they might find. He compares this to someone who won't check their bank account because they're scared of being broke, or won't go to the doctor because they're afraid of bad news. This kind of person isn't making an honest mistake - they're being dishonest with themselves, and that dishonesty will affect everything else they do.

Key Takeaway

Lewis's main point is that you can't separate living a good life from seeking truth. If Christianity might be true, you owe it to yourself to honestly investigate it rather than trying to ignore it. Being a "good person" requires first being an honest person who faces reality, even when it's uncomfortable. As Lewis puts it, you should choose to be a human being, not an ostrich hiding its head in the sand.
